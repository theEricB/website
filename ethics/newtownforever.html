Through the lens of foundational ethics, equal access and opportunity for the wider public is of chief concern when attempting to make a design accessible. Transportation methods in a highly public forum such as Newtown must cater to the community through providing a myriad of modes of transportation to be used easily and safely for participants of a broad spectrum of abilities.

First-hand observational behavioural analysis of the site informed the decision to focus on pedestrian and cycle networks within the area, identifying the sizable amount of traffic currently occupying the footpaths and car lanes along Newtown’s main road. The busy (and at times chaotic roads) within the suburb appear dangerous for cyclists who are forced to cycle among the cars on the roads, especially for less skilled riders. Observation of the area during peak-traffic times on both weekdays and weekends lead to the identification of strategies that would improve the lives and the general safety of occupants within Newtown. Ethically, the observation of the needs of the public in an indiscriminatory manner was an ethical consideration implemented by designers. The decision to create a shared motor-lane directly stems from this first-hand observational analysis of the traffic conditions, where the direction of heavy traffic was noted at different times of the day.

Ethically, in accepting a project such as this urban renewal, the architects essentially absorb the responsibility of meeting the needs of the public with no differential consideration or treatment. This means adequate room for motor vehicles, motor bicycles, public buses, bicycles, scooters, and pedestrians of differing ability levels should be considered thoroughly and in collaboration with one another. Whether it be due to financial, mental health or physical ability reasons, some individuals are not able to drive any form of motor vehicles; the inclusion of widened footpaths and clear, wide bicycle lanes is a relatively easy and low cost means of designing for the community and making Newtown a safe and welcoming suburb. Alternatively, for the same list of reasons, some people are only able to travel privately or publicly with motor vehicles. The project design remains feasible as it does not impede on anyone’s ability to travel using their preferred method. The addition of a shared motor lane could compensate for the increased traffic levels that may result from these changes, however. As someone with a number of disabled family members, I have witnessed first-hand how difficult and demoralising daily tasks can be for the disabled community when their needs are not accounted for in terms of transportation; and thus I feel strongly about an architect/urban designer’s ethical responsibility to cater for all ability levels.

An issue that hinders much of the potential for accessible design is the associated cost of designing universally. In a completely ethical world, this would be seen as a priority and thus warrant the expenditure no matter the cost. Unfortunately, this is not the case with majority of designs. When considering why this might not be so, debate has risen around whether those in positions of power in the design process value accessible design over cost efficiency. The contemporary capitalist agenda that drives most development regrettably does not place importance on inclusivity over profit.

In terms of striving for a resolution (or at least an improvement) in this area, perhaps we need to reconsider the traditional professional hierarchies that inform designs such as this one. How many (if any) of the voices that drive change part of the disabled community? Aiming to create the most diverse design team possible in all levels of such a hierarchy would greatly benefit many areas of design, both in ethical and functional terms. As discussed in class, diversity often breeds a broader scope of who the design can benefit in many areas, not only with accessibility standards in mind.

Another positive way forward could be the potential for the government subsidising of accessible and universal design. If the Australian government would afford more of a budget to organisations that strive towards inclusivity in design, perhaps the built environment could evolve to be a more accommodating space for all. This subsidy could come in the form of philanthropic donations, grants towards retrofitting existing and future buildings with accessible design and a multitude of other streams of change.

In summary, an array of foundational ethics questions and concerns are raised when considering accessible design within architecture as a profession. The deliberate and constant consideration of this is necessary to ensure contemporary designs cater for the wider community of all abilities. Doing so affords individuals their independence and dignity in navigating the built environment.
